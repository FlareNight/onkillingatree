About the Poet
Dr. Gieve Patel
(born 1940)
General Practitioner, Poet & Playwright from Mumbai
Dr. Gieve Patel is a renowned Indian poet, playwright, and practicing physician based in Mumbai. Born in 1940, he studied medicine at Grant Medical College and has maintained a career as a general practitioner while pursuing his passion for literature. His unique background combining medicine and art brings a distinctive sensibility to his work.
Patel's poetry is known for its profound connection to nature, the environment, and the human condition. His medical training provides precision and deep understanding of the body and mortality, which permeates his literary work. He has published several collections of poetry and plays, earning numerous accolades for his contributions to Indian English literature.

The Message: Reflections on 'On Killing a Tree'
Understanding the Message of 'On Killing a Tree'
Through 'On Killing a Tree,' Gieve Patel masterfully reveals the systematic brutality required to destroy something that takes years to grow. The poem transcends simple environmental commentary to become a meditation on violence, resilience, and the hidden vulnerabilities of life itself.
Key Themes
  • Slow Destruction vs. Quick Violence: The poem emphasizes that true destruction requires patience, systematic effort, and complete removal of life's source‚Äîa metaphor for environmental devastation.
  • Nature's Resilience and Fragility: Despite surface damage, the tree regenerates. But when the roots are destroyed, recovery becomes impossible‚Äîrevealing nature's dual capacity for resilience and vulnerability.
  • The Hidden Source of Life: Focusing on the roots‚Äîthe hidden, most sensitive part‚ÄîPatel reveals the invisible foundation of all life that lies beneath the surface.
  • Human Responsibility: The poem indicts human indifference and challenges us to recognize the gravity of our environmental choices and their consequences.
Discussion Points
  • What does "killing" the tree symbolize in our relationship with nature?
  • How does the poet's medical background influence the clinical precision of destruction described?
  • What is the significance of focusing on the root system as the ultimate target?
  • How does the poem's message apply to modern environmental challenges?
  • What personal responsibility do we have toward environmental conservation?
In an age of rapid deforestation and environmental crisis, 'On Killing a Tree' remains profoundly relevant. It calls us not just to appreciate nature's beauty, but to recognize our duty as custodians of life. Every tree, every ecosystem, every living thing requires our conscious protection and respect.
Stanza 1
It takes much time to kill a tree,
Not a simple jab of the knife
Will do it. It has grown
Slowly consuming the earth,
Rising out of it, feeding
Upon its crust, absorbing
Years of sunlight, air, water,
And out of its leprous hide
Sprouting leaves.
Stanza 2
So hack and chop
But this alone wont do it.
Not so much pain will do it.
The bleeding bark will heal
And from close to the ground
Will rise curled green twigs,
Miniature boughs
Which if unchecked will expand again
To former size.
Stanza 3
No,
The root is to be pulled out -
Out of the anchoring earth;
It is to be roped, tied,
And pulled out - snapped out
Or pulled out entirely,
Out from the earth-cave,
And the strength of the tree exposed,
The source, white and wet,
The most sensitive, hidden
For years inside the earth.
Stanza 4 (Final)
Then the matter
Of scorching and choking
In sun and air,
Browning, hardening,
Twisting, withering,
And then it is done.
